About

Built from the pain of building it all one-off

Every company needs the same pieces

I spent years consulting in CPG manufacturing -- in the warehouse, on the loading dock, in the quality lab with a hairnet and frock. Not advising from a conference room. Configuring systems, mapping processes, building integrations for the people who actually use them.

Across every company, the pattern was the same. They all needed the same infrastructure pieces: API gateways, authentication services, caching layers, data synchronization, event streaming, workflow engines. Every engagement started with rebuilding the same foundational infrastructure, customized just enough to feel unique but fundamentally identical to the last one.

Integration, authentication, caching, data sync -- I built custom versions of these over and over. Different companies, different ERPs, same core problems. The vendor solutions were bloated, expensive, and locked you in. The open-source alternatives required stitching together a dozen tools and hoping they played nice.

The real breaking point came with our SAP-DocuSign integration -- the kind of solution SAP sells as a product. The company had hired consultants who circled for months. I designed and programmed the complete architecture from the ground up, single-handedly, in 4 days: 40 ABAP components, 15+ database tables, 16 documentation files, 2 business integrations, OAuth authentication, real-time webhooks, automatic PO release. The kind of project consulting firms quote at $400K-$600K over 6-9 months.

That speed was not an accident. It was the result of having built and rebuilt the same patterns so many times that the architecture was already clear before the first line of code. And it made one thing obvious: these pieces should exist as a platform, not as one-off consulting deliverables.

So I stopped building them one-off and built the platform once, properly, in Rust.

Every company needs the same pieces. We stopped building them one-off.

-- Nathan Murray, Founder

AI + Expertise: A Force Multiplier

Some see AI coding tools and think "cheap labor replacement." We see a force multiplier in the hands of functional architects who understand both business operations and code structure. AI handles syntax specifics; the architect provides business logic, patterns, and architectural decisions. AI is excellent at generating code once you tell it what to build. It's terrible at understanding your business problem and knowing which SAP patterns actually work in production.

What we believe

Speed comes from building it right, not patching it together. Every shortcut in infrastructure becomes a long-term tax. We build properly the first time so you never pay that tax.

Working software beats sales decks. Decks can be polished. Demos can be scripted. But software either runs in your environment or it does not. We ship things that run.

Drop-in compatible beats proprietary lock-in. If you already speak Redis protocol, PostgreSQL wire format, or Kafka consumer groups, our products speak those too. Migrate in. Migrate out. Your choice.

One platform beats twenty vendors. Every vendor boundary is a potential failure point, a billing surprise, and a finger-pointing opportunity. We eliminated them.

Why "Forged"?

Forging is about shaping raw material into something useful through heat and pressure. It is craft -- taking hard problems and forming them into working solutions through sustained effort.

We forge enterprise infrastructure from raw Rust -- shaping it into tools that replace entire vendor stacks. Every product is hammered into shape by the same pressure that created the need: years of building one-off solutions and knowing there had to be a better way.

It is the opposite of theoretical. Forged things are real. They work.
